"\nText\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n|\n\n\n\n52013PC0228\n\nProposal for a REGULATION OF THE EUROPEAN PARLIAMENT AND OF THE COUNCIL on promoting the free movement of citizens and businesses by simplifying the acceptance of certain public documents in the European Union and amending Regulation (EU) No 1024/2012 /* COM/2013/0228 final - 2013/0119 (COD) */\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\nEXPLANATORY MEMORANDUM\n1.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Context of the proposal\n1.1\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 General context\nThe 2009 Stockholm Programme ''An open\nand secure Europe serving and protecting citizens''[1] stressed the\nimportance of making Union citizenship effective and put the citizens at the heart\nof EU policies in the area of justice. Its actions are focused on 'building a\ncitizens' Europe', including through promotion of citizens\u2019 rights, in\nparticular the right to freedom of movement. The related Action Plan[2] confirms this\nmandate and states that a well-functioning European judicial area \"should\nbe put at the service of citizens and businesses so as to support economic\nactivity in the single market (\u2026)\". Against this background, the\nAction Plan foresees the adoption of a legislative proposal for dispensing with\nthe formalities for the legalisation of public documents between the Member\nStates. In this context, in its Resolution on the Stockholm Programme, the\nEuropean Parliament considered that the priorities in the field of civil\njustice must first and foremost meet the needs expressed by individual citizens\nand businesses. Therefore, it \"calls for a simple and autonomous\nEuropean system for (\u2026) the abolition of requirements for legalisation of\ndocuments\".[3]\n\nIn response, the European Commission confirmed\nits commitment to facilitate the free circulation of public\ndocuments within the EU in its 2010 Citizenship Report[4] and presented in December 2010 a concrete vision to the public in the Green Paper on \"Less bureaucracy for\ncitizens: promoting free movement of public documents and recognition of the\neffects of civil status records''.[5]\nThrough that Green Paper, the Commission launched a consultation on the possible\nmeans to facilitate the use and acceptance of public documents between the\nMember States.\nAt the same time, the creation of\nthe EU Single Market received a new impetus with the adoption of the Single\nMarket Act[6],\nwhich aims at strengthening citizens' confidence in their internal market and\nmaximising its potential as the real growth engine within the Union's economy.\nThis implies, among others, the elimination of disproportionate barriers\nhampering the full enjoyment of the internal market freedoms by Union citizens\nand businesses. Fostering mobility of citizens and businesses across borders in the EU is also one of the main cornerstones of the\nSingle Market Act II[7]\nand a precondition to deliver on its potential. To this end, the Commission is\ndetermined to continue working towards its vision of a Single Market where\ncitizens and businesses are free to move cross-border\nwhenever and wherever they want to and without unjustified restrictions caused\nby diverging national rules. \nIn the same vein, the Commission's Action Plan\non European company law and corporate governance[8]\nfocuses on supporting European businesses, in particular\nas regards strengthening legal certainty for their cross-border operations. It is worth mentioning that the Digital Agenda\nfor Europe[9]\nrefers to the proposed legislation on electronic identification and eSignatures[10], which includes\nthe issue of administrative formalities related to these identification instruments.\nAccording to the recent Entrepreneurship 2020\nAction Plan[11],\nreducing excessive regulatory burden remains on the top of the Commission's\npolitical agenda. The Action Plan calls for the elimination or reduction of red\ntape whenever possible for all businesses and particularly for\nmicro-enterprises including the self-employed and liberal professions, who are\nexceptionally vulnerable to the burdens of bureaucracy due to their smaller\nsize and limited human and financial resources. Against this background, it\ncontains the commitment of the Commission to propose legislation abolishing\nburdensome authentication requirements for public documents which SMEs have to\nproduce to conduct cross-border business within the Single Market. This commitment\nsupports the objectives of the Europe 2020 Strategy for growth and jobs[12], in particular the\nimprovement of the business environment in Europe. \nAll the mentioned initiatives feed the determined\nEU contribution to move decisively beyond the financial and economic crisis.\nConsequently, cutting red tape, simplifying the procedures for\ncross-border use and acceptance of public documents between the Member States as\nwell as harmonizing the related rules contributes to all actions aimed at\nmoving towards the creation of a citizens' Europe and a well-functioning Single\nMarket for EU businesses. \nOn 25 May 1987, the Member States adopted the\nBrussels Convention abolishing the legalisation of documents between the Member\nStates of the European Communities. However, that Convention has not entered\ninto force, since it has not been ratified by all Member States but it is\napplied provisionally by six of them in relation to each other.\nNotwithstanding, the aim of the EU to be an area\nof advanced social and economic integration should enable citizens and businesses to fully benefit from the rights and freedoms\nguaranteed by the Treaties and the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, and\nentitle them to a simplification of their everyday life and business going\nbeyond the standards established by existing instruments of Union and international\nlaw. \nThis proposal is one of the key initiatives in\nthe European Year of Citizens 2013[13]\nand provides at the same time a concrete contribution to the policy of the 'Justice\nfor Growth'. The proposal draws inspiration and complements\nthe mentioned actions, initiatives and commitments of the Union in that it promotes the full enjoyment by Union citizens\nand businesses of their key rights attached to Union citizenship and internal\nmarket. It brings added value by establishing horizontal principles on the use\nand acceptance of public documents between the Member States, complementing\nexisting sectoral Union law in this area, filling the gaps in those areas which\nremain currently unregulated by Union law and supporting EU initiatives aimed\nat simplifying the life of citizens and business conditions for economic\noperators[14].\nIn parallel, the proposal promotes the principle of mutual trust between the\nauthorities of the Member States, because it improves their knowledge on public\ndocuments of other Member States and also creates administrative cooperation\nbetween authorities. \n1.2\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Grounds and objectives of\nthe proposal\nThe mobility of Union citizens is a practical reality, evidenced in\nparticular by the fact that some 12 million of them study, work or live in\nanother Member State of which they are not nationals. This mobility is\nfacilitated by the rights attached to the citizenship of the European Union: in\nparticular the right to free movement and, more generally, the right to be\ntreated like a national in the Member State of residence. These rights\ndemonstrate and promote a better understanding of the value of European integration.\nThe same is true for EU businesses,\nin particular SMEs. Nearly half of them are involved in some sort of\ninternational contact and a no less important number makes regularly use of the\ninternal market freedoms through cross-border business transactions or\nclientele in various Member States. \nDespite the fact that the right to freedom of movement and residence\nas well as the internal market freedoms are firmly anchored in primary Union\nlaw and substantially developed in secondary Union law, a gap still remains\nbetween the existing legal rules and the reality confronting citizens and businesses when they seek to\nexercise those rights in practice. \nToday, if Union citizens and businesses exercise\ntheir free movement rights or internal market freedoms by, for example,\nchoosing to reside or do business in another Member State, they face a series\nof difficulties when presenting the necessary public documents to the\nauthorities and getting them accepted by that Member State contrary to its own\nnationals and businesses. The common function of these documents is to establish evidence of\nfacts recorded by an authority. In most cases they are presented in order to\nobtain access to a right, to receive a service or to comply with an obligation.\nEven when these documents are fully\nlegal and unproblematic in their country of origin, the\ncitizens and businesses still have to undergo disproportionate and burdensome administrative formalities to\nprove their authenticity in the other Member State. This is often a source of frustration and irritation, and is not\nconducive to achieving a citizens' Europe.\nThe administrative formalities in question are legalisation and\nApostille, required to establish the authenticity of public documents so that\nthey can be used outside the Member State where they have been issued. They\nconcern particularly the authenticity of the signature and the capacity in\nwhich the person signing the document has acted. Other formalities which serve a\nsimilar purpose in cross-border situations are certification requirements for\ncopies and translations.\nLegalisation and Apostille are characterised by a legal framework\nthat is fragmented because it is based on several sources: national laws that\ndiffer considerably from one another; a number of international multilateral\nand bilateral conventions which have been ratified by a varied and limited\nnumber of countries and which are unsuitable when it comes to providing the\nsolutions needed to ensure the free movement of Europeans; fragmented EU law\nwhich deals only with certain limited aspects of the matters raised. The result\nis a lack of clarity and a regulatory framework which does not provide the\nlegal certainty European citizens and businesses need to cope with matters that have a direct impact on\ntheir everyday lives.\nAll the identified formalities require administrative steps and\ninvolve some loss of time and a quite considerable cost which varies greatly\nfrom one Member State to another. Furthermore, they do not necessarily prevent fraud\nand forgery of public documents. As a result, they can be considered outdated\nand disproportionate mechanisms for ensuring the wished objectives of legal\nsecurity. More effective, secure and simpler mechanisms or systems should be\nidentified, which would allow to consolidate mutual trust and to promote closer\ncooperation between the Member States within the Single Market, in particular\nas regards a more effective prevention of fraud and forgery of public documents.\nThe existing linguistic obstacles are exacerbated by the absence of\nmultilingual standard forms at Union level for the most frequently used public\ndocuments between the Member States. \nDifficulties in terms of costs and time arise also for national\npublic administrations.\nTo summarise, there are several problem drivers justifying the need for\nEU action: \n1.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 increased intra-EU mobility\nof Union citizens and businesses facing\nthe identified administrative formalities, which cause cost and loss of time; \n2.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 indirect discrimination of\nnationals of other Member States in comparison with own nationals in\ncross-border scenarios;\n3.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 fragmented legal framework\nat EU and international level as regards legalisation, Apostille and\nadministrative cooperation;\n4.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 shortcomings of existing\nEU and international laws concerning the cirulation of public documents.\nThese problem drivers are analysed in detail in the\nImpact Assessment accompanying the proposal. \nThe general objective of this proposal of a\nhorizontal nature is to simplify the identified\nadministrative formalities in order to facilitate and enhance the exercise of\nthe Union citizens' right to free movement within the EU and of businesses'\nright to freedom of establishment and freedom to\nprovide services within the Single Market, whilst upholding the general public\npolicy interest of ensuring the authenticity of public documents.\nMore concretely, the proposal aims at: \n\u00b7                        \nreducing practical difficulties\ncaused by the identified administrative formalities, in particular cutting the related red tape, costs and delays; \n\u00b7                        \nreducing translation costs related to the free\ncirculation of public documents within the EU;\n\u00b7                        \nsimplifying the fragmented legal framework\nregulating the circulation of public documents between the Member States;\n\u00b7                        \nensuring a more effective level of detection of\nfraud and forgery of public documents;\n\u00b7                        \neliminating risks of discrimination among Union\ncitizens and businesses.\nThe proposal is streamlining the rules and\nprocedures applied currently between the Member States concerning the\nverification of authenticity of certain public documents and at the same time complementing\nthe existing sectorial Union law, including rules relating to the circulation\nof specific public documents, by abolishing the requirements of legalisation, Apostille\nand simplifying the use of copies and translations. It draws\ninspiration from the existing sectorial Union law and relevant international\ninstruments, whilst reinforcing confidence in public documents issued in other\nMember States. The proposal does not, on the other hand, modify the existing sectorial\nUnion law which contains provisions on legalisation,\nsimilar formality, other formalities or administrative cooperation, but complements it. \n1.3\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Scope and legal effects\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 \n1.3.1 Scope\nThe scope of this proposal covers public documents issued by authorities\nof the Member States and having formal evidentiary value relating to birth,\ndeath, name, marriage, registered partnership, parenthood, adoption, residence,\ncitizenship, nationality, real estate, legal status and representation of a\ncompany or other undertaking, intellectual property rights and absence of a criminal\nrecord. Documents drawn up by private persons and documents issued by authorities\nof third States are excluded from its scope. \n1.3.2 Legal effects\nThis proposal is a follow-up to the 2010 Commission Green Paper concerning\nadministrative formalities which serve the purpose of authentication of public\ndocuments between the Member States. It focuses on\nestablishing the authenticity of public documents, drawing inspiration from the\nexisting EU law and relevant international instruments. It is important to\nstress that the proposal does not address the issue of recognition of effects\nof public documents between the Member States nor does it introduce full\nharmonisation of all public documents existing in the Member States or\nsituations in which they are needed in cross-border scenarios by EU citizens\nand businesses.\nThe Union multilingual standard forms to be established by this\nproposal will not produce legal\neffects as regards the recognition of their content in the Member States where\nthey will be presented. These standard forms will not prejudice the use of equivalent\npublic documents drawn up by authorities of the issuing Member State. When used,\nthey will have the same formal evidentiary value as their national equivalents\nas regards their authenticity. Their primary purpose will be the reduction of\nthe remaining translation requirements for Union citizens and businesses.\n2.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Results\nof consultations with the interested parties and impact assessment\nAn extensive consultation has taken place with the interested\nparties, Member States and the public, in particular following the adoption of\nthe Commission Green Paper. The Commission has received altogether a high\nnumber of contributions from Member States, national and international\nprofessional organisations as well as from citizens. As described above, this\nproposal relates exclusively to the first part of the Green Paper \"the\nfree movement of public documents\". The second part regarding \"the recognition\nof the effects of civil status records\" is not addressed in the current\nproposal.\nIt results from the contributions that the majority of the Member\nStates and of the stakeholders welcomed the Commission's intention to abolish the\nadministrative formalities relating to the verification of authenticity of\npublic documents. However, they underlined the need to introduce accompanying safeguards,\nsuch as the possibility to verify the authenticity of public documents through strengthened\nEU-wide administrative cooperation in order to facilitate the transition from\nthe current system to the new framework and ensure legal certainty and minimize\nfraud.\nThe Commission continued to meet and consult with relevant\nstakeholders in 2012 to complete and update the contributions to the first part\nof the Green Paper. Meetings were held with, amongst others, the International\nCommission on Civil Status (ICCS), the Hague Conference on Private\nInternational Law (HCCH), the Council of the Notariats of the European Union (CNUE),\nthe European Association of Registrars (EVS), the European Association of\nCraft, Small and Medium Enterprises (UEAMPE), the European Land Registry\nAssociation (ELRA) and the International Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Trans and\nIntersex Association (ILGA).\nA meeting with Member States Experts was held on 27 September 2012\nto discuss a working paper containing the main elements of the proposal. The\nCommission continued to discuss these elements with experts from several Member\nStates.\nThe Commission has also carried out a thorough Impact Assessment\nwhich accompanies the proposal. \n3.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Legal\nelements of the proposal\n3.1\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Summary of the proposed\naction\nThe proposal establishes a clear set of horizontal rules exempting\npublic documents falling under its scope from legalisation or similar formality\n(Apostille). In also foresees simplification of other formalities related to\nthe cross-border acceptance of public documents, namely of certified copies and\ncertified translations. In order to guarantee the authenticity of public\ndocuments which circulate from one Member State to another, it introduces an effective\nand secure administrative cooperation based on the Internal Market Information\nSystem (\"the IMI\"), established by Regulation (EU) No 1024/2012 of\nthe European Parliament and of the Council of 25 October 2012.[15] The IMI includes also a\nfunctionality to maintain a repository of model templates of public documents used\nwithin the Single Market that can serve as first checking point of unfamiliar\ndocuments. \nThe proposal also establishes Union multilingual standard forms concerning\nbirth, death, marriage, registered partnership and legal status and\nrepresentation of a company or other undertaking. In addition, with the aim to further\nreduce the remaining translation requirements for EU citizens and businesses, such\nUnion multilingual standard forms could be established at a later stage for\npublic documents relating to name, parenthood, adoption, residence, citizenship\nand nationality, real estate, intellectual property rights and absence of a\ncriminal record. The Union multilingual standard forms should not be mandatory\nbut when used they have the same formal evidentiary value as the simular public\ndocuments drawn up by the authorities of the issuing Member State. \nThe main features of the proposal can be summarised as follows:\n3.1.1\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Subject matter and Scope\n(Articles 1 and 2)\nThe proposal promotes the free movement of citizens and companies or\nother undertakings by dispensing certain public documents issued by authorities\nof the Member States from the accomplishment of all forms of legalisation or of\nsimilar or other formalities related to their acceptance in other Member States when presented to\ntheir authorities. It also provides for the establishment\nof Union multilingual standard forms concerning birth, death, marriage, registered\npartnership and legal status and representation of a company or other\nundertaking.\nIt covers public documents which are issued by authorities of a\nMember State and which have to be presented to authorities of another Member\nState. The proposal does not deal with the recognition of the content of public\ndocuments issued by the authorities of the Member States. \nThe proposal concerns situations in which the identified public\ndocuments are required in cross-border scenarios by: (i) public authorities of\nthe Member States or (ii) entities of the Member States tasked by virtue of an\nact or administrative decision to carry out public duties.\n3.1.2\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Definitions (Article 3)\nThe proposal contains the definitions of the following terms: 'public\ndocuments', 'authority', 'legalisation', 'similar formality', 'other formality'\nand 'central authority'. In particular, it stipulates that the term 'public\ndocuments' means only documents issued by authorities of a Member State and\nhaving formal evidentiary value relating to birth, death, name, marriage and\nregistered partnership, parenthood, adoption, residence, citizenship and\nnationality, real estate, legal status and representation of a company or other\nundertaking, intellectual property rights, and absence of a criminal record.\n3.1.3\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Exemption from legalisation\nor similar formality (Article 4)\nUnder the proposal, the public documents originating from the Member States and falling\nunder its scope are exempted from all forms of legalisation or similar\nformality foreseen by the Hague Convention of 1961 abolishing the requirement\nof legalisation for foreign public documents when presented to the authorities\nof other Member States, as a general principle.\n3.1.4\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Simplification of other formalities\n(Articles 5 and 6)\nUnder the proposal, authorities cannot\nrequire parallel presentation of the original of a public document and of its\ncertified copy issued by the authorities of other Member States. In addition,\nauthorities have to accept a non-certified copy if the original document is\npresented together with such a copy and furthermore they have to accept\ncertifications issued in other Member States.\nThe proposal foresees that authorities should\naccept non-certified translations of public document issued by the authorities\nof other Member States. If the authorities of the Member State in which the\npublic document is presented have reasonable doubt about the correctness or\nquality of its translation in an individual case, they may require a certified\ntranslation of that document.\n3.1.5\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Request for information in\ncase of reasonable doubt (Article 7)\nThe proposal foresees that if the authorities of the Member State in\nwhich the public document or its certified copy are presented have reasonable\ndoubt, which cannot be otherwise resolved, concerning their authenticity, in\nparticular as to the authenticity of the signature, the capacity in which the\nperson signing the document has acted, or the identity of the seal or stamp,\nthey may submit a request for information to the relevant authorities of the\nMember State where these documents were issued. In case a particular national\nauthority does not have access to the Internal Market Information System, it\nmay request information from the central authority of its Member State under\nthe procedure laid down by that Member State. When the central authority is\nunable to respond to such request, it shall transmit the request to the central\nauthority of the Member State where the document was issued. The requested\nauthorities should reply to such requests within the shortest possible period\nof time which should not take longer than one month at maximum.\n3.1.6\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Administrative cooperation\n(Articles 8, 9 and 10)\nThe proposal stipulates that the Internal\nMarket Information System should be used for the purposes of requests for\ninformation in cases of reasonable doubt about the authenticity of public\ndocuments as well as about their certified copies. The Internal Market\nInformation System is a software application accessible via Internet, developed\nby the Commission in cooperation with the Member States in order to assist\nMember States with the practical implementation of information exchange\nrequirements laid down in Union acts, such as in this Regulation. It allows\ncollecting templates of national public documents in its repository that will\nalso help the authorities, including on linguistic aspects, by making them\nfamiliar with the documents of other Member States. \nFurthermore, the proposal contains detailed\nrules concerning the designation, functions and meetings of the central\nauthorities. Among others, the central authorities provide and regularly update best practices on the prevention of\nfraud of public documents.\n3.1.7\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Union multilingual standard\nforms (Articles 11, 12, 13, 14 and 15)\nThe proposal establishes Union multilingual\nstandard forms in all official languages concerning birth, death, marriage, registered\npartnership and legal status and representation of a company or other\nundertaking contained in its Annexes I, II, III, IV and V respectively. The Union\nmultilingual standard forms will be available to citizens and companies or\nother undertakings parallel and as an alternative to the national public\ndocuments on a voluntary basis and given the same formal evidentiary value as the\nsimular public documents issued by the authorities of the issuing Member State.\nIf a Union multilingual standard form has been established for a particular\npublic document, the authorities of a Member State must issue such form upon\nrequest in case an equivalent public document exists in that Member State. The\nquestion which authorities issue the forms falls under the national law of each\nMember State. It must be issued under the same conditions (e.g. as regard the\nfees) as the equivalent public document existing in that Member State. These standard forms do not produce legal effects as regards the\nrecognition of their content in the Member States where they will be presented.\nTaking into account the increasing use of modern\ncommunication technologies in the area of public documents, the Commission will\ndevelop electronic versions of Union multilingual standard forms or other\nformats suitable for electronic exchange and will encourage the Member States\nto make them available to Union citizens and companies or other undertakings.\nThe Commission will develop detailed\nguidance on the issuance of Union multilingual standard forms in cooperation\nwith central authorities of the Member States.\n3.1.8\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Relations with others instruments\n(Articles 16, 17 and 18)\nThe proposal does not prejudice the\napplication of Union law which contains provisions on legalisation, similar\nformality or other formalities nor the application of\nUnion law on electronic signatures and electronic identification. Finally, the proposal\ndoes not prejudice the use of other systems of administrative cooperation\nestablished by Union law which provide for exchange of information between the\nMember States in specific areas (e.g. CCN/CSI system in the area of taxation and customs).\n3.1.9\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Review (Article 21)\nThe Commission should evaluate the\napplication of the Regulation every three years and prepare a report,\naccompanied by proposals for amendments. In this context, the Commission should\nin particular consider if the scope of the Regulation should be extended to\nfurther categories of public documents. In addition, the Commission should\nreflect the benefits of proposing of further Union multilingual standard for\npublic documents relating to name, parenthood, adoption, residence, citizenship\nand nationality, real estate, intellectual property rights and absence of a\ncriminal record, or to categories falling under the potentially extended scope.\n3.2\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Legal\nbasis\nThis proposal is based on Article 21(2) TFEU\nwhich empowers the European Parliament and the Council to adopt provisions with\na view to facilitating the exercise of the rights of Union citizens to move and\nreside freely within the territory of the Member States, subject to the\nlimitations and conditions laid down in the Treaties and by the measures\nadopted to give them effect. Administrative obstacles to the cross-border use\nand acceptance of public documents have a direct impact on the free movement of\ncitizens. Thus, removing these obstacles would facilitate the exercise of the free\nmovement of citizens as foreseen in Article 21(2) TFEU. \nThis Article is combined with Article\n114(1) TFEU which empowers the European Parliament and the Council to adopt\nmeasures for the approximation of the provisions which have as their object the\nestablishment and functionning of the internal market. Administrative obstacles\nto the cross-border use and acceptance of public documents have a direct impact\non the full enjoyment of the internal market freedoms of EU businesses as described in\nArticle 26(2) TFEU and referred to in Article 114(1) TFEU. It is therefore the suitable\ncomplementary legal basis to cover public documents used by EU businesses in cross-border\nscenarios within the internal market.\nThe measures provided for in Article 21(2)\nand Article 114(1) TFEU are adopted by means of the ordinary legislative\nprocedure laid down in Article 294 TFEU, and after consulting the Economic and\nSocial Committee as regards measures foreseen under Article 114(1) TFEU.\nA Regulation appears to be the most\nappropriate legislative form of the proposal, taking into account both the\nproblems and objectives identified. \n3.3\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Subsidiarity and Proportionality\n3.3.1.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Subsidiarity principle\nThis proposal complies with the\nrequirements of subsidiarity.\nThe problems outlined above and in the Impact\nAssessment accompanying this proposal have a clear cross-border dimension and\ncannot by their nature be properly dealt with at the level of the Member States.\nAny unilateral action by the Member States would run counter to the objective of legal certainty and predictability\nfor citizens and economic operators and would further increase the existing\nlegislative patchwork. In addition, the Member States are unable to offer\neffective solutions to the relating challenges due to their EU dimension. An EU\nlevel action would help Union citizens and businesses to use different categories of public documents\nin cross-border situations without disproportionate and burdensome\nadministrative formalities. For these reasons, an EU action would ensure higher\nefficiency. The adoption of a directly applicable simplifying measure\ncontaining horizontal principles on the free circulation of public documents\nbetween the Member States as well as the establishment of Union multilingual\nstandard forms demonstrate the clear added value of such an action. \n3.3.2.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Proportionality\nprinciple\nThe proposal complies with the principle of\nproportionality in that it is strictly limited to what is necessary to achieve\nits objectives. It does not try to harmonise the public documents of the Member\nStates or rules governing their circulation within the EU. The proposal focuses\nonly on the abolition or simplification of the identified administrative\nformalities, with the necessary accompanying elements to enable the verification\nof authenticity of public documents in cases of reasonable doubt. \nThe\naccompanying Impact Assessment demonstrates that the benefits of the key\nelements of this proposal outweigh the costs and that the proposed measures are\nproportionate.\n3.4\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Impact\non Fundamental Rights\nIn accordance\nwith the Strategy for the effective implementation of the Charter of\nFundamental Rights by the European Union[16], the Commission has ensured that the proposal\ncomplies with the rights set out in the Charter and more importantly, that it\nfurther promotes their application.
